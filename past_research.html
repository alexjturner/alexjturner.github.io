Spatial extent of source influences on satellite observations (Click here to return to Current Research)

I spent my junior and senior years of undergrad (2011-2012) working on a project with Daven Henze at CU Boulder quantifying the impact of distant emissions on satellite observations of short-lived atmospheric species. An common assumption when estimating emissions of short-lived species is that the observed satellite columns can be directly related to the emissions because the lifetime is sufficiently short. The aim of this project was to test this assumption using a novel adjoint-based method.


Fig. Time series progression of the sensitivity groupings, g1 and g2, and local source contribution of NH3 in a column in Europe.

Objectives:

  • Develop an adjoint-based method for assessing the impact of non-local emissions on a satellite column
  • Quantify the impact of non-local emissions on satellite columns of short-lived species

Collaborators:

Daven Henze (CU Boulder), Randall Martin (Dalhousie), & Amir Hakami (Carleton)

References:

  • Turner, A.J., D.K. Henze, et al. (2012), The spatial extent of source influences on modeled column concentrations of short-lived species, Geophys. Res. Lett. | pdf

Support:

University of Colorado Discovery Learning Center award to Alex Turner and a NASA Applied Sciences Program grant to Daven Henze.


Midlatitude cyclones and air quality (Click here to return to Current Research)

I spent the summer of 2011 working at the Geophysical Fluid Dynamics Lab (GFDL) with Arlene Fiore on a project examining the long-term interactions of climate change and air quality. Tropospheric ozone is an important topic particularly in the Northeastern US where a large fraction of counties have traditionally been out of attainment (EPA 2006). Previous work had indicated that ozone exceedances in the Northeastern US were strongly correlated with cyclone frequency. We examined the natural variability in cyclone frequency, examined how that might change going forward, and the implications for air quality.


Fig. Cyclone frequencies in the GLST for future warming scenarios. (a) The three ensemble members and ensemble mean of cyclone frequencies in the moderate warminc scenario (RCP 4.5). Base case (2006-2025) and future case (2081-2100) are shown in blue and orange, respectively, with a line connecting the means. (b) The timeseries of cyclone frequency in the GLST for an extreme future warming scenario (RCP 8.5). There is a statistically significant (p < 0.01) decreasing (-0.06a-1 cyclones/summer) trend in the extreme warming scenario.

Objectives:

  • Examine the long-term cyclone frequency
  • Quantify the impact of cyclone frequency on past, present, and future air quality

Collaborators:

Arlene Fiore (GFDL; now at Columbia), Mike Bauer (NASA GISS), & Larry Horowitz (GFDL)

References:

  • Turner, A.J., A.M. Fiore, et al. (2013), Summertime cyclones over the Great Lakes Storm Track from 1860-2100: variability, trends, and association with ozone pollution, Atmos. Chem. Phys. | pdf

NOAA Hollings Scholarship to Alex Turner.


Evaluating mesoscale meteorology for atmospheric transport (Click here to return to Current Research)

I spent the summer of 2010 working at Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratories with Marc Fischer on the CalGEM Project. The goal of the project was to quantify methane emissions in California using in situ observations. My role in the project was to simulate meteorology using the Weather Research and Forecasting (WRF) model that would be used to drive a particle dispersion model and evaluate the meteorology. I found a discrepancy between the observed and modeled boundary layer heights in California's Central Valley during the summer. This discrepancy began after the farmers started irrigating their crops in early June and persisted through the summer. WRF-simulated boundary layer heights generally agreed well with the observations in other seasons. This implies that the WRF land surface model was misrepresenting the balance between sensible and latent heat fluxes in the summer because it does not properly account for irrigation.


Fig. Figure shows a marked decrease in observed boundary layer heights at three radar wind profiler sites in California after farmers started irrigating their crops in early June. This decrease was not seen in the WRF-simulated boundary layer heights.

Objectives:

  • Quantify methane emissions in California
  • Generate meteorology that can be used to drive a particle dispersion model
  • Evaluate the meteorology with available observational records

Collaborators:

Marc Fischer (LBNL) & Seonguen Jeong (LBNL)

Support:

DOE GCEP/SURE award to Alex Turner.
